# HOW TO USE THIS SKILL

When a user asks a startup question, FIRST run the intake questionnaire below.
Then answer using the distilled frameworks from all 20 lectures.
Always cite which speaker/principle you are drawing from.
Apply advice specifically to the user's stated situation — not generically.

---

# INTAKE QUESTIONNAIRE
Run these questions before giving advice. Ask them conversationally, not all at once.

## Round 1 — The Idea
- What does your product do in one sentence?
- What specific problem does it solve?
- Who exactly is your customer (be precise: not "businesses" but "Tamil Nadu moneylenders")?

## Round 2 — The Market
- Who are your competitors? How do they fail users?
- How big is your immediate target market (not the total addressable market)?
- Why does this market exist NOW — what changed recently to make it possible?

## Round 3 — Your Advantage
- What makes you 10x better than the existing solution on at least one key dimension?
- Why are YOU the right person to build this?
- What do you know that others don't?

## Round 4 — Current Status
- Pre-idea / Building / Launched?
- Number of users / revenue / growth rate?
- Solo founder or team? Technical or non-technical?

# PART 1: THE IDEA

## What Makes a Good Idea (Sam Altman, L1)
- Ideas matter. "A bad idea is still bad. Great execution toward a terrible idea gets you nowhere."
- Best ideas look terrible at first — if they sounded great, too many people would be working on them.
- You want: **unpopular but right**. Something that sounds like a bad idea but is actually a good one.
- The idea should come FIRST, startup second. Only start when you feel compelled by a specific problem.
- Best companies are mission-oriented. You need conviction to survive 10 years of pain.
- Key test: "Why now?" Why couldn't this be done 2 years ago, and why will 2 years from now be too late?
- Build something YOU need. If not, get so close to customers you practically live with them.

## How to Get Startup Ideas (Paul Graham, L3)
- **Don't try to think of startup ideas.** That produces ideas that sound plausible but are fake.
- Instead: live at the leading edge of some technology. "Live in the future, then build what's missing."
- Best ideas start as side projects — your conscious mind would reject them as too weird.
- Work on hard problems that interest you, with people you like. The startup idea emerges naturally.
- Trust your instincts about people. Be skeptical of people who seem impressive but feel wrong.
- **Gaming the system stops working with startups.** There's no boss to trick — only users who either want your product or don't.

## Market Sizing (Sam Altman, L1-2)
- Care more about **growth rate of market** than current size.
- A small, rapidly growing market beats a big, slow-growing one every time.
- Investors focus on current market size — this is their mistake. You're betting on where the market will be in 10 years.
- Students have an edge here: you have better instincts about which technologies young people are adopting.

# PART 2: MONOPOLY STRATEGY

## The Master Formula (Peter Thiel, L5)
**Business Value = X (value created) × Y% (value captured)**
X and Y are completely independent. Airlines create huge X but capture tiny Y. Google creates smaller X but captures huge Y. Google wins financially.

## The Only Two Types of Business (Peter Thiel, L5)
- **Perfect competition**: race to zero profits. Everybody loses money. Airlines, restaurants.
- **Monopoly**: durable, compounding wealth. Google, Microsoft, Apple.
- Almost nothing in between. The gap is huge but hidden because everyone lies.
- Monopolists pretend to be in brutal competition (to avoid regulation). Competitors pretend to be unique monopolies (to attract capital).

## The Lies People Tell (Peter Thiel, L5)
- **Non-monopolist lie**: "We're the only British restaurant in Palo Alto." (Artificially narrow market — intersection framing)
- **Monopolist lie**: "We're not a search engine, we're an advertising/technology company." (Artificially broad market — union framing)
- The buzzword startup pattern ("mobile social sharing app") is almost always a red flag.

## How to Build a Monopoly (Peter Thiel, L5)
**Start absurdly small.** Dominate a tiny market completely, then expand concentrically.
- PayPal: started with 20,000 eBay power sellers. Got 25-30% market share in months.
- Facebook: started with 10,000 Harvard students. 60% market share in 10 days.
- Amazon: started with just books.
- Going after a giant market on Day 1 = you haven't defined your category correctly.

## The 4 Pillars of Monopoly (Peter Thiel, L5)
1. **Proprietary technology**: 10x better than next best thing on some key dimension
2. **Network effects**: hard to start, but self-reinforcing once critical mass reached
3. **Economies of scale**: high fixed cost, near-zero marginal cost (software is perfect)
4. **Brand**: lodges in minds — real phenomenon, but weakest moat alone

## First Mover vs Last Mover (Peter Thiel, L5)
- Don't obsess over being first — **be last**. The definitive, enduring player in a category.
- Microsoft (OS), Google (search), Facebook (social networking if it holds).
- Most tech company value lives far in the future: 75-85% of PayPal's 2001 value came from 2011+.
- Silicon Valley **overvalues growth rates**, **undervalues durability**.
- The real question: "Why will this still be the leading company in 15 years?"

# PART 3: PRODUCT

## The Most Important Thing (Sam Altman, L1)
- Build something a **small number of users love**, not something a large number like.
- It is much easier to expand from love to more love, than from lukewarm to love.
- If you get this right, you can get other things wrong. If you get this wrong, nothing else matters.
- Signs it's working: **organic word-of-mouth growth**. If you're explaining why lack of growth is okay, you have a real problem.

## Building Product (Sam Altman, L1)
- Start simple. First version of Google: one textbox. First Facebook: comically basic.
- Simplicity forces you to do one thing extremely well.
- Be fanatical about quality. Founders should feel physical pain when the product is bad.
- Don't put anyone between yourself and your users in the early days.
- Metrics to track: growth, active users, retention, revenue, NPS. **Never** total registrations.

## Minimum Viable Product (Adora Cheung, L4)
- MVP = smallest feature set that solves the problem. But "viable" matters — don't ship something flat.
- Before building: storyboard the full user experience. How does customer find you, sign up, use it, return?
- One-liner matters: "Get your place cleaned for $20/hour" beats a paragraph of explanation.
- **Temporary brokenness is better than permanent paralysis.** Don't optimize for edge cases early.
- Beware the Frankenstein approach: don't build every feature users request. Find the underlying problem.

## Talk to Users (Adora Cheung, L4; Emmett Shear, L16)
- Get out of the building. User feedback in person beats surveys 10x.
- **Honesty curve**: strangers who pay give the most honest feedback. Your mom gives the least.
- Track retention cohorts: what % of Month 1 users are still active at Month 3, 6, 12?
- Ask: "Would you be really bummed if this company went away?" That's the real signal.

## User Interviews (Emmett Shear, L16)
- Who you talk to is as important as what you ask.
- **Don't show them your product** in early interviews. You want what's in their head, not reactions to yours.
- Don't ask "Would you use this feature?" People say yes. Get them to pay instead.
- Ask about behavior: "How do you solve this today?" "How often does this problem happen?" "What would your ideal solution look like?"
- **Shut up.** Best sales/research calls have you talking 30% of the time.
- After 6-8 interviews you stop learning new things. Then build.
- Compare users vs non-users vs competitor-users. Non-users give the most important insights for expanding the market.
- Record interviews. Play them back for your team — more persuasive than any memo.

## Products Users Love — The Relationship Framework (Kevin Hale, L7)
- Think of acquiring users like **dating** (first impressions, origin story) and retaining users like a **marriage** (long-term investment).
- First impressions are pass/fail. A weird 404 page, a boring signup form — these are seduction opportunities.
- Use "enchanting quality" (miryokuteki hinshitsu) not just "functional quality" (atarimae hinshitsu).
- **Support-Driven Development**: make everyone do customer support. Developers who support their own code fix bugs immediately.
- John Gottman's Four Horsemen (relationship killers) apply to startups: criticism, contempt, defensiveness, **stonewalling** (not responding to users = biggest churn driver).
- Three paths to market dominance: best price (logistics), best product (R&D), or **best overall solution** (customer intimacy). Only the third is accessible to any startup at any stage.

# PART 4: GROWTH

## The Only Thing That Matters for Growth (Alex Schultz, L6)
**Retention is the single most important thing.** If your retention curve doesn't flatten (become asymptotic), no growth tactic will save you.
- Check retention before doing ANY growth work. If curve slopes to zero, fix the product first.
- "If you don't have product-market fit, don't hire a growth hacker. Don't do virality."

## North Star Metric (Alex Schultz, L6)
- Every company needs one metric everyone rallies around.
- Facebook: Monthly Active Users. WhatsApp: Sends. Airbnb: Nights booked. eBay: Gross Merchandise Volume.
- Pick the metric that represents real value delivered, not vanity (registrations ≠ actives).
- The Magic Moment: identify the single experience that hooks a user. For Facebook it was "seeing your friend's face." Get users there as fast as possible.
- Optimize for the **marginal user** (people who almost didn't stay), not yourself.

## Three Types of Growth (Adora Cheung, L4)
1. **Sticky growth**: get existing users to return (deliver great experience, measure CLV and retention cohorts)
2. **Viral growth**: users refer others (optimize touch points, referral mechanics, conversion flow)
3. **Paid growth**: CLV > CAC. Break even within 3-12 months max. Beyond 12 months = dangerous territory.

## Doing Things That Don't Scale (Walker Williams, Stanley Tang, L8)
- **DoorDash** launched with a landing page (1 hour to build), PDF menus, and personal cell phone. No tech. Found product-market fit before building anything.
- **Teespring**: first users required days of meetings, free design, personal social media. Still worth it.
- First users are always hardest. Think of it as pushing a boulder uphill — steepest at the start.
- Don't give product away free — it deprives you of validation and real feedback.
- Turn users into **champions** by delighting them with something unexpected.
- Do things that don't scale **as long as possible**. Don't give it up willingly — it should be ripped from you.

## Virality Framework (Alex Schultz, L6)
Sean Parker's model: **Payload × Conversion Rate × Frequency = Virality**
- Hotmail: low payload (one email at a time), high frequency, high conversion → viral
- PayPal: gave money for referrals → high conversion compensated for low payload/frequency
- Facebook: pure word-of-mouth — not viral via mechanics, viral via quality

## Tactical Growth Channels (Alex Schultz, L6)
- **SEO**: keyword research first (demand, supply, value), then links (authority), then internal link distribution.
- **Email/SMS/Push**: deliverability first. Notifications > newsletters. Triggered/contextual > broadcast. Never spam power users.
- **Internationalization**: Facebook internationalized too late. Prioritize languages by where growth is going, not where it is now.
- General principle: a good plan executed fast beats a perfect plan tomorrow.

# PART 5: TEAM & HIRING

## Cofounders (Sam Altman, L2)
- **#1 cause of early startup death at YC: cofounder blowup.**
- Pick cofounders you know well — ideally years. College or prior work is ideal.
- Never pick a random cofounder just because you both want to start something.
- Better to be solo than have a bad cofounder. But solo is still bad.
- 2-3 cofounders is ideal. 5 is very bad.
- Every cofounder MUST have vesting (standard: 4 years, 1-year cliff). Pre-negotiate what happens if someone leaves.
- Near-equal equity split. Disproportionate splits are a red flag that founders aren't being honest with each other.

## Hiring (Sam Altman, L2)
- **Try not to hire.** Every person you add increases complexity. Be proud of how little you can get done with.
- When you do hire: it must be your #1 priority. The cost of a bad early hire is existential.
- Airbnb spent 5 months interviewing their first employee. Hired only 2 in year 1.
- Source through personal referrals. Best companies are built on personal networks for first 100 employees.
- Three questions for any hire: Are they smart? Do they get things done? Do I want to spend a lot of time around them?
- Work with them on a project before hiring. You'll learn more than any interview.
- Call references deeply: "Top 5% of people you've worked with?" "Would you hire them again? Why not?"
- Give ~10% equity to first 10 employees. Founders are almost always too stingy with employees, too generous with investors.

## What to Look For (Patrick Collison, Ben Silbermann, L11)
- Stripe's first 10: genuine/straight, complete things, cared deeply (offended when something is slightly off)
- Pinterest's first hires: hard-working, high integrity, low ego, creative, curious, quirky — people who want to build something great
- Look for people who are **undervalued by the market**: early career, not yet discovered, willing to bet on themselves
- First 10 hires are actually 100 hires — each person brings 10 more like them

## Firing (Sam Altman, L2; Sam Altman, L20)
- **Fire fast.** Everyone waits too long. It's bad for the company and bad for the employee.
- Fire people creating office politics or who are persistently negative — visible to whole company, toxic.
- Fire people getting every decision wrong consistently, not people who make occasional mistakes.
- When firing: preserve their dignity publicly. Don't trash them — everyone at your company will call them for references.

# PART 6: EXECUTION

## Focus and Intensity (Sam Altman, L2)
- **The secret**: extreme focus + extreme dedication. You can have a startup and one other thing. Not many things.
- Each day: identify the 2-3 most important things and do ONLY those. Ignore/delegate/defer the rest.
- Growth and momentum are the lifeblood of startups. If you lose momentum, everything falls apart.
- If momentum sags: don't give speeches about vision. Find small wins first. "Sales fix everything."
- Don't worry about competitors until they're beating you with a real, shipped product.
- The best founders respond to email fastest, make decisions fastest, show up in person.

## The CEO's Job — Editing (Keith Rabois, L14)
Think of your job as an editor, not a writer:
1. **Simplify**: eliminate everything. You can change the world in 140 characters.
2. **Ask clarifying questions**: narrow to the 1-2 things that actually matter.
3. **Allocate resources**: move people/money toward what matters most.
4. **Ensure consistent voice**: every touchpoint (website, support, recruiting) should feel like one person.
5. **Use less red ink over time**: measure yourself by how often you have to correct.

## Barrels vs Ammunition (Keith Rabois, L14)
- Most people are **ammunition** (good at executing tasks).
- **Barrels** can take an idea from conception to shipped, bringing others along.
- One barrel is worth 10 engineers. Add ammunition only after you have more barrels.
- How to identify barrels: give tiny responsibilities, expand until it breaks. Watch whose desk people go to for help.

## One Thing at a Time (Keith Rabois, L14)
- Peter Thiel at PayPal: every person had exactly ONE thing. Enforced strictly.
- People solve B+ problems (things they know how to solve) instead of A+ problems (high impact, hard).
- If you give everyone one thing, the A+ problems actually get solved.

## Metrics and Anomalies (Keith Rabois, L14)
- Build a dashboard. Every employee should use it daily.
- **Pair indicators**: measure fraud rate AND false positive rate together, not just one.
- Look for anomalies, not expected data. PayPal found eBay power sellers this way — became their entire market.
- Transparency: share board decks with all employees after every meeting. Glass walls on conference rooms.

## Management Basics (Ben Horowitz, L15)
**Core lesson**: When making critical decisions, see it through the eyes of EVERYONE — not just the person in front of you.
- A demotion looks win-win between two people but can destroy morale for the whole team.
- Giving raises when asked signals everyone should ask constantly — even if it feels fair in the moment.
- **Process protects culture.** Formal review cycles aren't bureaucracy — they're fairness infrastructure.
- One-on-ones: weekly (or every 2 weeks max). Agenda set by employee, not manager.
- Performance feedback must be frequent and honest. First-time CEOs are bad at this. Over-compensate.

# PART 7: CULTURE

## Why Culture Matters (Alfred Lin, Brian Chesky, L10)
- Culture = core values × actions × mission.
- Companies with strong cultures outperform S&P 500 by nearly 2x over 20 years.
- Culture needs to be **designed**, not allowed to happen accidentally.
- Write down your core values before you hire employee #1 (Airbnb did this — almost uniquely).
- Hire AND fire based on values. Separate "values interviewers" who aren't in the functional area.

## Creating Core Values (Alfred Lin, L10)
- Start with what matters most to you personally. What kinds of people do you hate working with? Take the opposite.
- Values must be credible (you actually live them) and unique (tied to your specific mission).
- Generic values (honesty, teamwork) mean nothing. "Be a cereal entrepreneur" (Airbnb) = scrappiness + creativity under constraint.
- Go deep: "teamwork" isn't enough — what does that mean at YOUR company in YOUR situation?

## Culture at Scale (Patrick Collison, Ben Silbermann, John Collison, L11)
- Stripe: radical internal transparency. Everyone on every mailing list. Builds trust and alignment.
- Pinterest: autonomous mini-teams with designer + engineer + writer + PM. Self-contained, fast.
- Culture is the invariant as you stop being able to make individual decisions directly.
- Onboarding matters: get new people shipping/in real meetings on Day 1. Push them off the cliff.
- Give frequent, direct feedback on cultural fit especially. People coming from normal companies need to learn your culture.

# PART 8: FUNDRAISING

## The Right Mindset (Marc Andreessen, Ron Conway, Parker Conrad, L9)
- **Be so good they can't ignore you.** The best pitch improvement is making your company better.
- Raising venture capital is the EASIEST thing you'll ever do as a founder. Recruiting, sales, growth — all harder.
- Bootstrap as long as you can. Raising money is not success. It's just permission to do harder things.
- The "onion theory of risk": each round peels away one layer of risk. Raise to achieve milestones, not to stockpile.

## What Investors Look For (Ron Conway, Marc Andreessen, L9)
- Is this person a leader? Are they focused and obsessed?
- Does the idea come from a personal problem they experienced?
- Can they communicate it clearly in one sentence?
- Are they decisive? Procrastination kills startups.
- Invest in strength, not lack of weakness. Great companies often have serious flaws. The ones without flaws often aren't remarkable.

## Fundraising Tactics (Michael Seibel, L19)
**30-second pitch** (3 sentences):
1. What your company does (simple enough for your mom)
2. How big the market is
3. Your traction / growth rate

**2-minute pitch** adds:
4. Your unique insight (the AHA — what you know that smart people don't)
5. Business model (say it clearly, don't hedge)
6. Team (accomplishments only; how you met; all full-time)
7. The Ask (know your terms: safe/note, cap, amount, minimum check)

**When to fundraise**: when you're strong (traction, momentum), not weak (desperate). Create competitive pressure — schedule all meetings in one week.

## Seed vs Series A (Marc Andreessen, Ron Conway, L9)
- Seed: sets price later (convertible note/safe). Fast, simple. Best for early stage.
- Series A: price set. Usually 20-30% dilution. Should follow seed round.
- Best introduction: through entrepreneurs who were just written about in press → they introduce you to that reporter's VC contact.
- Choosing investors = choosing a 10-15 year partner. More important than valuation by far.
- Watch out for: unsophisticated investors (your uncle), excessive dilution (>25% in seed), destroyed cap tables.

# PART 9: SALES

## Sales Fundamentals (Tyler Bosmeny, L19)
- As a founder, **you are the sales team.** Your passion and product knowledge trump sales experience.
- Only 2.5% of companies will even take your call as an early startup. It's a numbers game.
- **Prospect**: personal network → conferences (get attendee list, pre-book meetings) → cold email (short: who I am, what I'm building, can we talk tomorrow?)
- **On the call: shut up.** Ask questions. Let them talk 70% of the time. "Why did you agree to take my call?"
- **Follow up relentlessly.** Even enthusiastic customers need 10+ follow-ups to close.
- Get to YES or NO fast. "Maybes" kill startups. A no is better than a maybe.
- Avoid the free trial trap. Offer instead: "Annual agreement with 30-day out clause."
- Don't build features that one customer "needs before they'll sign." It almost never works.

## PR (Justin Kan, L8)
- PR is not a growth engine — it's a bootstrap. Can get first 100 users but doesn't scale.
- Types of stories: product launch, fundraise, milestones, business stories, stunts, contributor articles.
- Get introduced through entrepreneurs who were recently covered → they introduce you to that reporter.
- Give reporters 1 week+ of lead time. Get face-to-face meetings. Prepare a bulleted story outline.
- Build relationships with 3-4 key journalists directly. Better than any PR firm.
- Don't get excited by your own press. It feels like success but often isn't.

## Enterprise Sales (Aaron Levie, L12)
- Enterprise IT spending: $3.7T/year vs consumer app market of ~$170B. The opportunity is real.
- What changed: cloud computing, mobile devices, consumerization of IT — lower the barrier for new entrants.
- Start intentionally small: find the wedge (one painful problem), own it, expand.
- Do things incumbents can't or won't: platform-agnostic, unusual business models (Zenefits charges insurance companies, not customers).
- Find customers on the bleeding edge of their industry — they'll co-create the product with you.
- Listen to customers but don't build exactly what they ask. Translate problems into best solutions.
- Your product should sell itself — but you still need sales to help customers navigate.

# PART 10: BEING A GREAT FOUNDER

## The Founder Mindset (Reid Hoffman, L13)
- No founder is great at everything. You need 1-2 superpowers, not a complete skillset.
- Great founders navigate paradoxes: you must be both flexible AND persistent. Know when to hold and when to pivot.
- Have an **investment thesis**: written bullets of why your idea is right, what you know others don't.
- Increase or decrease confidence in that thesis based on data. Pivot when confidence decreases for a sustained period.
- Go where the right networks are. Location matters. Silicon Valley is optimal for software/mobile/marketplace. Not for fashion or some other verticals.
- Being contrarian is only valuable if you're also **right**. Ask: what do I know that smart people who disagree with me don't?

## Founder Paradoxes (Reid Hoffman, L13)
- Work on the product 100% AND recruit 100% — sometimes both simultaneously.
- Have belief (long-term vision) AND fear (short-term paranoia about execution).
- Focus internally (build) AND externally (recruit, network, fundraise).
- Long-term vision AND solving today's immediate problem.
- "Founders have no balance." This is a sprint. Do it fully or not at all.

## How to Operate as Company Scales (Sam Altman, L20)
**Transition point: ~25 employees.** Job shifts from "build great product" to "build great company."

Common failure modes as founders become managers:
1. **Afraid to hire senior people** — executives who've done it before are worth it. Every founder wishes they'd done this earlier.
2. **Hero mode** — doing everything yourself instead of hiring. You will burn out.
3. **Bad delegation** — telling people what to do and checking in. Real delegation = "You decide. I trust you."
4. **Poor personal organization** — no system for tracking what everyone is working on.

Write down:
- **HOW you do things** (processes) → becomes law as company scales
- **WHY you do things** (values) → becomes culture as company scales

## Psychology of Founding (Sam Altman, L20; Dustin Moskovitz, L1)
- Startups are genuinely hard. Don't start for glamour, to be the boss, or for flexibility — you get none of those.
- As you succeed: highs get better, lows get worse. The swing expands. Manage this.
- You go from underdog (everyone roots for you) to target (haters, press criticism). Make peace with it early.
- The final cause of death for most startups: **the founders give up.** Manage your psychology so you don't quit when you shouldn't.
- **Focus.** The most common post-YC failure: founders get distracted by conferences, advising, etc. Focus made you successful. Protect it.

# PART 11: LEGAL & FINANCIAL MECHANICS

## Formation (Kirsty Nathoo, Carolynn Levy, L18)
- Incorporate as a **Delaware C-Corp**. Always. Don't get fancy.
- Use Clerky or similar service. Don't pay lawyers to do what online tools do for $200.
- Keep ALL signed documents in a safe place. This will matter during due diligence.
- File an **83(b) election** within 30 days of stock purchase. Missing it cannot be undone.

## Equity & Vesting (Kirsty Nathoo, Carolynn Levy, L18)
- **Execution > idea.** Don't give disproportionate equity to whoever "had the idea."
- In top YC companies: zero instances of significantly disproportionate founder equity splits.
- Standard vesting: 4 years, 1-year cliff. Every cofounder must have this.
- All founders must be paid. Working for free is illegal and creates leverage for fired cofounders.
- **File 83(b) election.** Cannot be overstated. Deals have died because of missing this.

## Raising Money Mechanics (Kirsty Nathoo, L18)
- Unpriced rounds (convertible notes/safes): fast, no immediate dilution, investor not yet a shareholder.
- Understand your future dilution before you take money. $2M on $6M cap = investors own ~25% before Series A.
- Only raise from **accredited, sophisticated investors** who understand long time horizons.
- Read every term. Board seats, pro-rata rights, information rights — all matter.
- Keep your cap table clean. A destroyed cap table (outside investors own 80%) = VCs walk away.

## HR as You Scale (Sam Altman, L20)
- Performance feedback must be formal, frequent, tied to compensation.
- Use **compensation bands** for fairness. Someday everyone will know everyone else's comp.
- Give significant equity to employees every year (3-5% of company total, ongoing).
- **Refresher grants**: always stay ahead of people's vesting cliffs. Never let someone have vested 3 of 4 years with no new grant.
- New HR rules kick in at 50 employees: sexual harassment training, diversity training, etc.

# PART 12: HARDWARE / IOT PRODUCTS

## Building Hardware Differently (Hosain Rahman, L17)
- Hardware requires: amazing hardware + world-class software + intelligent data layer. All three equally.
- Hardware teams and software teams move at different speeds. Integration requires deliberate friction management.
- Constraints are opportunities: "What is the user problem that, once solved, people can't live without?"
- Don't ask users "Would you pay $200 for a wireless speaker?" — ask about their behavior and needs.
- Define **WHYS** before building: what problem does this solve, why does it matter, where is it going in 3 years?
- Think in systems, not features. Hardware + app + data + platform = the product.
